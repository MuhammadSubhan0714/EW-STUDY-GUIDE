7 C's of Communication
Focus: Completeness & Correctness

Exam Focus
Your syllabus specifies Completeness and Correctness only. This section covers all 7 so you understand context, but exam questions will test these two most heavily.
#CCore IdeaKey Rule
1Completeness ★All facts the reader needs for the desired reactionAnswer every question; give the 5 W's + H
2ConcisenessShortest possible without losing meaningCut wordy phrases; no repetition
3ConsiderationWrite from the reader's perspective ("you-attitude")Focus on "you," not "I/we"
4ConcretenessSpecific, vivid, definite — not vagueUse facts, figures, and active verbs
5ClaritySimple words, clear structureOne idea per sentence; avoid jargon
6Correctness ★Grammatically and factually accurateRight grammar, spelling, format, tone level
7CourtesyTactful, respectful, non-discriminatoryAvoid negative phrasing; use euphemisms

1 — Completeness in Detail

A message is complete when it contains all facts the reader needs. Use the 5 W's + H checklist:

  • Who — Who is involved / who should act?
  • What — What is the subject / action required?
  • When — What is the deadline / time?
  • Where — Location / venue?
  • Why — Reason / purpose?
  • How — Method / instructions?
✗ Incomplete
Hi everyone, just a reminder about the meeting tomorrow. See you then!
✓ Complete
Hi everyone, this is a reminder about tomorrow's meeting on the new telecommuting policy. The meeting will be held on Thursday, 22 May, at 10:00 a.m. in Conference Room B, 2nd Floor. Please confirm attendance by replying to this email.

6 — Correctness in Detail

Correctness means no grammatical, spelling, or factual errors. It also means choosing the right level of writing.

LevelWhen to UseExample
FormalOfficial letters, reports, emails to superiors"I would like to request…"
InformalColleagues you know well"Could you send me that file today?"
SubstandardNever in professional writing"gonna," "cuz," "tbh"
Common Correctness Errors (spot & fix these)
"conservation" → should be "conversation"  |  "two-weak deadline" → should be "two-week deadline"  |  Comma splice: "I finished, I submitted" → "I finished; I submitted."

Spot-the-Error Practice

Q1: Identify the errors and rewrite: "Hi, Thanks so much for meeting me at lunch today! I enjoyed our conservation. I'm sure the two-weak deadline won't be an issue." +
Errors: "conservation" → "conversation" (wrong word); "two-weak" → "two-week" (wrong homophone).

Corrected: "Hi, Thank you for meeting me for lunch today. I enjoyed our conversation, and I look forward to working together. I am confident the two-week deadline will not be an issue."
Q2 (Concreteness): Rewrite: "The manager was late for the meeting." +
Answer: "The manager, Mr. Asif, arrived 25 minutes late to the 9:00 a.m. board meeting on Monday."

Why? Specific name, exact time delay, specific meeting — replaces vague "late."
Q3 (Conciseness): Shorten: "I have conducted an investigation to study the marketing strategies of all our competitors." +
Answer: "I have investigated our competitors' marketing strategies."

Cuts 8 words to 7 — remove "conducted an investigation" (wordy verb phrase) and "to study" (redundant purpose).
Q4 (Consideration): Rewrite with "you-attitude": "I am pleased to inform you that we have approved your loan application." +
Answer: "Your loan application has been approved. You may collect the documents from our branch at your convenience."

Why? Shifts focus from "I/we" to "you." The reader is the centre.

Letter Writing
Full Block Format

Key Rule — Full Block Format
In Full Block Format, every part of the letter is aligned to the left margin. No indentation anywhere. Paragraphs are separated by a blank line.

The 11 Components of a Business Letter

#PartNotes
1Letterhead / Sender's AddressYour company name + address. Skip if using printed letterhead.
2DateFull format: 20 December 2023 or December 20, 2023
3Inside AddressRecipient's name, title, company, and address.
4SalutationDear Mr. Khan: (formal = colon)  |  Dear Ahmed, (standard = comma)
5Subject Line (optional)RE: Training Session — May 2024
6Opening ParagraphState purpose immediately. No "I am writing to…" padding.
7Body Paragraph(s)One idea per paragraph. Support + details.
8Closing ParagraphAction required / next step / goodwill.
9Complimentary CloseSincerely, / Regards, / Yours faithfully,
10Signature Block4 blank lines → handwritten signature → typed name + title
11Enclosures / CCEnc. / CC: [Name] at the very bottom if needed

Direct vs. Indirect Approach

ApproachWhen to UseStructure
DirectGood news, neutral requests, routine lettersMain point → Details → Goodwill close
IndirectBad news, refusals, negative announcementsBuffer → Reasons → Bad news → Alternative/Goodwill
Indirect Approach — BRAB Formula
Buffer → Reason → Adverse news (softened) → Beneficial close

Sample Letter — Indirect Approach (Bad News)

Scenario from your past paper: You work in IT at a hotel. Your boss arranged a training session on "Using Social Media in the Hospitality Industry." You cannot attend. Write a negative news letter using the Indirect approach, Full Block Format.

Muhammad Subhan IT Department, The Pearl Continental Hotel Club Road, Karachi 75500 22 May 2024 Mr. Tariq Hussain Head of IT The Pearl Continental Hotel Club Road, Karachi 75500 Dear Mr. Hussain: RE: Training Session — Social Media in the Hospitality Industry The upcoming training session on using social media in the hospitality industry is an excellent initiative, and I appreciate your efforts in organising this professional development opportunity for our department. Unfortunately, an urgent network outage at our Lahore branch requires my immediate on-site presence on that same date. The system failure has disrupted operations for over 50 hotel staff, and I am the only certified engineer available to resolve the issue. Leaving this unresolved would result in significant financial and operational losses for the company. I sincerely regret missing this valuable session. I would appreciate it if you could share the training materials or recording so that I may benefit from the content and apply the knowledge to my role. Thank you for your understanding and continued support. Sincerely, Muhammad Subhan IT Engineer, Pearl Continental Hotel Ext: 3412 | m.subhan@pchotel.com
Examiner's Checklist — Letter Marks Breakdown
Format (Full Block, all components present): 5 marks  |  Content (correct approach, logical flow): 10 marks. Always verify: date format, colon after salutation, 4-line gap for signature, subject line.

Practice Questions

PQ1: What are the components of a Full Block Format business letter? List them in order. +
1. Sender's Address / Letterhead  →  2. Date  →  3. Inside Address  →  4. Salutation  →  5. Subject Line (optional)  →  6. Opening Paragraph  →  7. Body  →  8. Closing Paragraph  →  9. Complimentary Close  →  10. Signature Block  →  11. Enc./CC (if any).

In Full Block Format: everything is flush left. No indentations.
PQ2: You are a manager. Write a letter denying an employee's request for a pay raise. Use Indirect approach. +
Structure to follow:
Buffer: "Thank you for your continued dedication to the team and for taking the initiative to discuss your compensation…"

Reasons: "As you may be aware, the company is currently undergoing a period of budget consolidation following a challenging Q4. Salary adjustments across all departments have been temporarily placed on hold until the new financial year…"

Adverse News (softened): "In light of these circumstances, we are unable to process a salary increment at this time."

Beneficial Close: "We will revisit compensation packages in the upcoming annual review in January 2025. Your performance continues to be valued, and we encourage you to speak with HR about our performance-linked bonus scheme, which remains active."

Persuasive Messages

What Is a Persuasive Message?

A persuasive message is designed to influence an audience's attitudes, beliefs, or behaviours — convincing them to take action, adopt a viewpoint, or support an idea.

The AIDA / ANSVA Structure

StepNamePurposeExample Hook
AAttentionGrab the reader with a powerful opening"Did you know 80% of startups fail due to poor communication?"
NNeedIdentify the problem or desire"Managing remote teams without structure leads to missed deadlines and frustration."
SSatisfactionPresent your solution"Our communication platform bridges that gap instantly."
VVisualizationPaint the picture of success (or failure)"Imagine your team hitting every deadline, from anywhere in the world."
AActionTell them exactly what to do"Sign up for a free 14-day trial today — no credit card needed."

The Three Pillars of Persuasion (Aristotle)

E
Ethos — Credibility
Establish trust. Use your authority, expertise, or reputation. "As a certified engineer with 10 years of experience…"
P
Pathos — Emotion
Appeal to feelings — hope, fear, empathy, pride. Charity ads showing families in need use pathos.
L
Logos — Logic
Use facts, statistics, and reasoning. "Studies show a 40% increase in productivity with structured schedules."

Additional Persuasion Techniques

TechniqueHow It WorksExample
Social ProofOthers are doing it — so should you"Join over 1 million satisfied users."
ScarcityLimited availability creates urgency"Only 3 seats left at this price!"
ReciprocityGive something first to earn a favourFree trial → paid subscription

Common Logical Fallacies (Know These for Exams)

FallacyWhat It DoesExample
Ad HominemAttacks the person, not the argument"You can't trust his opinion — he failed Math."
Straw ManMisrepresents the opponent's view"People who want clean energy just want to ban all cars."
False DilemmaOnly two options presented (when more exist)"You're either with us or against us."
Slippery SlopeOne step leads inevitably to catastrophe"If we allow calculators, students will never learn math."
Bandwagon AppealTrue because everyone believes it"Everyone is using this app, so it must be best."
Hasty GeneralizationBroad conclusion from limited evidence"My friend got food poisoning there once — it's terrible."
Post HocA follows B, so B caused A"I wore red socks and aced the exam — they're lucky!"
Circular ReasoningConclusion supports the premise"This policy is right because it is correct."
Red HerringDistracts from the real issue"Why worry about traffic when people are hungry?"

Sample Persuasive Email — Proposal Request

Subject: Proposal for a Dedicated Study Space in the CS Building Dear Dr. Malik, Engineering students at NUCES work with complex projects that demand uninterrupted focus, yet our current library closes at 8:00 p.m. — hours before most of us finish our assignments. Studies from MIT and IBA Karachi show that students with access to 24-hour study facilities score 18% higher on technical exams. A dedicated, after-hours room in the CS building would directly address this gap for over 500 enrolled students. Imagine a space where teams can collaborate on final-year projects without competing for library seats or working in noisy cafeterias. This investment would enhance academic performance and reflect positively on NUCES's reputation. We respectfully request a 15-minute meeting at your convenience to discuss the feasibility of this proposal. A preliminary cost estimate and floor plan are attached. Sincerely, Student Representative — CS Batch 2025

Practice Questions

PQ3: Identify the logical fallacy — "If students are allowed to skip one lecture, they'll start skipping all of them and eventually drop out." +
Slippery Slope. It assumes one small step (skipping one lecture) will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome (dropping out) without evidence for each step in between.
PQ4: You are writing a persuasive letter to your department head requesting a new lab computer. Write the "Attention" and "Need" paragraphs using ANSVA. +
Attention: "Did you know that 73% of CS students at top universities work on machines with at least 16 GB of RAM? Our students are competing in the same job market with computers that were purchased in 2016."

Need: "The current machines in Lab 4 frequently crash during compilation of large codebases, causing students to lose unsaved work and miss assignment deadlines. This directly impacts academic performance and student satisfaction scores."

Product Review Writing

What Makes a Good Product Review?

A product review is an evaluative piece that balances personal experience with objective assessment. It informs readers and helps them make purchasing decisions.

CRAFT Formula for Product Reviews
Context (who you are, how you use it) → Ratings summary → Advantages (pros, specific) → Flaws (cons, honest) → Takeaway (final verdict + who it's for)

Structure of a Product Review

SectionWhat to IncludeLength
IntroductionProduct name, category, price range, your context of use2–3 sentences
Key FeaturesSpecifications and standout features — be specific, not vague1 paragraph or list
PerformanceHow it worked in real use — results, speed, reliability2 paragraphs
Pros & ConsBalanced, honest list — minimum 3 eachBulleted list
VerdictScore/rating, final recommendation, target audience2–3 sentences

Sample Product Review — Laptop

★★★★☆ HP Pavilion 15 (Core i5, 16GB RAM, 512GB SSD) Price Range: PKR 95,000 — 110,000 INTRODUCTION I purchased the HP Pavilion 15 three months ago for use as a CS student at NUCES. My workload includes programming in C++, running virtual machines, and compiling large codebases daily. KEY FEATURES Processor: Intel Core i5-1235U (12th Gen) | RAM: 16 GB DDR4 Storage: 512 GB NVMe SSD | Display: 15.6" FHD IPS (250 nits) Battery: 41 Whr | Weight: 1.75 kg PERFORMANCE The laptop handles everyday programming tasks and multi-tab browsing without noticeable lag. Compiling a mid-size C++ project takes approximately 12 seconds — acceptable for the price range. The NVMe SSD boot time of 8 seconds is excellent. However, sustained workloads cause the fan to become audibly loud within 10 minutes, and the chassis heats noticeably around the keyboard area during long sessions. PROS: • Fast SSD significantly improves compilation and boot times • 16 GB RAM handles multiple IDEs and a browser simultaneously • IPS panel offers accurate colours — good for UI design work • Affordable for its specifications in the Pakistani market CONS: • Thermal performance poor under sustained load • 250-nit display is dim in outdoor / bright-room conditions • Battery life averages 4.5 hours — below the advertised 8 hrs • Build quality feels plastic-heavy at this price VERDICT | 8 / 10 The HP Pavilion 15 is a capable, budget-friendly laptop for CS students who work primarily indoors. Recommended for programmers and students — less ideal for video editors or those who travel frequently with the laptop unplugged.

Language Tips for Reviews

  • Use specific quantities: "boots in 8 seconds" not "boots fast"
  • Use hedged language for opinions: "in my experience," "appears to"
  • Balance positives and negatives — purely positive reviews lose credibility
  • Write for the reader's use case, not just your own
  • Avoid filler: "This product is amazing!" → "Delivers reliable performance under daily academic workloads"
PQ5: Write a 3-sentence introduction for a review of the Samsung Galaxy A55 aimed at NUCES students. +
"I have been using the Samsung Galaxy A55 for two months as my primary device for studies and social connectivity at NUCES Karachi. Priced around PKR 85,000, it targets mid-range buyers who want a flagship-like experience without the flagship price tag. As a student who relies heavily on camera quality for documentation and all-day battery life for on-campus use, I put this phone through rigorous daily testing."

Negotiation Skills

What Is Negotiation?

Negotiation is a process of dialogue between two or more parties to reach a mutually acceptable agreement. In professional communication, it involves persuasion, compromise, and strategic communication.

Key Negotiation Concepts

ConceptMeaningExample
BATNABest Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — your fallback optionIf the supplier won't lower price, you'll switch to a competitor
ZOPAZone Of Possible Agreement — the range where both parties can agreeYou'll pay PKR 50K–70K; they'll accept 60K–80K → ZOPA = 60K–70K
AnchoringSetting the first offer to frame the negotiationQuoting PKR 120K when you expect to settle at 95K
Win-WinBoth parties gain value — ideal outcomeBulk discount in exchange for long-term contract
Win-LoseOne party gains at the other's expenseForcing a take-it-or-leave-it offer

Stages of Negotiation

  1. Preparation — Research, know your BATNA, set objectives
  2. Opening — Set the tone, build rapport, state your position
  3. Exploration — Ask questions, understand the other party's needs
  4. Bargaining — Make and counter offers, use concessions strategically
  5. Closing — Reach agreement, confirm in writing
  6. Follow-up — Implement terms, maintain relationship

Negotiation Language Bank

FunctionUseful Phrases
Opening position"Our initial offer is…" / "We are looking for…"
Making a concession"We could consider… if you are able to…"
Rejecting politely"That falls outside what we can accommodate, however…"
Seeking clarification"Could you elaborate on what you mean by…?"
Reaching agreement"I think we have a basis for moving forward…"
Stalling for time"I'll need to check with my team before confirming."
Negotiation vs. Persuasion
Persuasion is one-directional — you convince someone to adopt your position.
Negotiation is bilateral — both parties move toward a middle ground. Effective negotiators use persuasive techniques within negotiation.
PQ6: What is BATNA and why is it important in a negotiation? +
BATNA stands for Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. It is the most favourable outcome a party can achieve if current negotiations fail.

It is important because: (a) it gives you a clear "walk-away" point, preventing you from accepting a bad deal; (b) the stronger your BATNA, the greater your negotiating power; (c) knowing the other party's BATNA helps you understand how far they will negotiate.
PQ7: You are negotiating a salary with a potential employer. Write 3–4 sentences demonstrating effective negotiation language. +
"Thank you for the offer of PKR 80,000 per month. Based on my research into market rates for this role and my 2 years of relevant internship experience, I was hoping for something closer to PKR 95,000. I understand budget constraints may exist, so I am open to discussing other components such as performance bonuses, flexible hours, or an accelerated six-month review. Could we explore what flexibility exists within the overall package?"

The 5 Stages of the Interview Process

  1. Icebreaker — Greetings, first impression, small talk
  2. Personal Qualifications — Skills, experience, accomplishments
  3. Organisation & Position — Your knowledge of the company
  4. Your Questions — You ask about the role and team
  5. Close & Follow-up — Thank the panel; send a thank-you note within 24 hrs

The STAR Technique (Behavioural Answers)

S
Situation
Describe the context and background. Keep it brief — 1–2 sentences.
T
Task
What was your specific responsibility or challenge?
A
Action
The steps YOU took. Use "I," not "we." This is the meatiest part.
R
Result
Measurable, positive outcome. Quantify where possible.

Standard Interview Questions & Model Answers

"Tell me about yourself." — How should you structure this? +
Structure: Present → Past → Future (1–2 minutes max)

Model Answer: "I am a final-year Computer Science student at NUCES Karachi, specialising in software engineering. Over the past two years, I have completed two internships — one at a fintech startup where I built REST APIs, and another at a logistics company where I optimised their database queries, reducing load time by 35%. I am now looking for a full-time role where I can apply my backend development skills to real-world problems, and your company's focus on scalable systems is exactly the environment I am hoping to grow in."
"Describe your most challenging academic project." (from your 2023 paper) +
Using STAR:
S: "In my third semester, my team of four was tasked with building a fully functional hospital management system in eight weeks using Java and MySQL."

T: "I was responsible for the database architecture and API integration, while also coordinating team progress as the unofficial project lead."

A: "When two team members fell ill mid-project, I redistributed tasks, created a priority-based schedule, held daily 20-minute stand-up calls, and put in extra hours to cover the backend. I also documented everything systematically so the team could resume quickly."

R: "We submitted on time and received a distinction — 92/100. My professor cited our documentation as one of the strongest submitted that semester."
"Why do you think you are the most suitable candidate for this internship?" (from your 2023 paper) +
"I believe I am a strong fit for three reasons. First, my technical skills align precisely with the role — I have worked with the exact stack you use (Python, Django, PostgreSQL) in both coursework and a freelance project. Second, I bring an ability to work under pressure: I have consistently met deadlines across intensive CS semesters at NUCES while maintaining a GPA above 3.5. Third, I am genuinely invested in your company's mission — I have followed your product development blog for six months and came into this interview with three ideas for features that could improve user retention. I would bring that same proactive attitude to the internship."

Qualities Employers Seek (Examiner Favourites)

Communication Teamwork Problem Solving Initiative Adaptability Integrity Drive for Results Leadership Commercial Awareness Enthusiasm

What Creates a Bad Impression

  • Poor personal appearance / not dressed professionally
  • Negative attitude — making excuses, criticising previous employers
  • Lack of preparation — unable to answer basic company questions
  • Failure to give concrete examples of skills (never say "I am a team player" without proof)
  • Over-emphasis on salary in early rounds
  • Giving one-word "yes/no" answers
PQ8: Match — "An interview in which a candidate is required to perform a task." +
Working Interview (also called Practical Interview). The candidate demonstrates actual job skills live — e.g. a coding test, a sample presentation, or a writing exercise.
PQ9: Match — "An interview conducted to separate deserving candidates from the less proficient ones." +
Screening Interview. This is typically an initial HR phone call or short online test used to filter the applicant pool before detailed interviews begin.
PQ10: Match — "An interview in which a candidate is given an imaginary situation and asked how they would act." +
Situational Interview. Example question: "What would you do if you discovered a critical bug in the system one hour before a major product launch?"

Q1 — 7 C's of Communication (20 marks)

(a) Rewrite the following 5 sentences to make them more Courteous and Considerate. [5 marks] +
Original → Rewritten:

1. "Your report is not good." → "The report would benefit from more specific data and clearer recommendations. I would be happy to discuss revisions with you."
2. "Every person should submit his CNIC." → "All employees are kindly requested to submit their CNIC to the secretary by 27 May."
3. "You have failed to get the contract." → "Unfortunately, the contract was awarded to another firm this time. We appreciate your effort and look forward to future opportunities."
4. "It is impossible to open an account today." → "Accounts opened after 3:00 p.m. are processed the next business day. You are welcome to complete your registration tomorrow morning."
5. "We don't refund if the item is soiled." → "Refunds are available for items returned in their original condition within 7 days of purchase."
(b) Make the following 5 sentences more Concrete. [5 marks] +
1. "The manager was late for the meeting." → "Mr. Raza arrived 20 minutes late to the 10:00 a.m. budget review on Thursday."
2. "We spent a lot of time on this project and need more." → "We have invested 340 hours over six weeks and require an additional two weeks to complete testing."
3. "Many students missed the exam due to rains." → "Forty-three students were absent from the Thursday morning exam due to the heavy monsoon rainfall."
4. "Due to insufficient staff, we could not install software on every computer." → "With only 2 technicians available, we installed the software on 18 of the 30 computers in Lab 3."
5. "The teacher gave the student negative feedback." → "Ms. Nadia told the student that his 500-word report lacked a clear thesis and contained four grammatical errors."
(c) Make the following 5 wordy sentences Concise. [5 marks] +
1. "I have conducted an investigation to study the marketing strategies of all our competitors." → "I investigated our competitors' marketing strategies."
2. "The report is an attempt to identify the causes behind the collapse of the bridge." → "The report identifies the causes of the bridge collapse."
3. "This manual was prepared to aid our dealers in being helpful to customers." → "This manual helps our dealers assist customers."
4. "It is the responsibility of our Production Department to meet the requirements of Sales." → "Production must meet Sales requirements."
5. "In spite of the fact that it's a longer commute, I made the decision to accept the job." → "Despite the longer commute, I accepted the job."
(d) Identify and explain the logical fallacy: "Everyone at this office uses Brand X pens, so they must be the best pens available." [5 marks] +
Fallacy: Bandwagon Appeal

This fallacy assumes that because a majority of people do something or use something, it must be correct, superior, or true. Popularity is used as a substitute for evidence.

Why it is flawed: The fact that all office members use Brand X pens does not objectively prove they are the best pens. It may simply mean the company purchases them in bulk for cost reasons, or staff have never been offered an alternative. Quality must be evaluated on objective criteria such as durability, ink quality, and ergonomics — not group usage.
